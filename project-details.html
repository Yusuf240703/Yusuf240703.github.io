Design & Manufacture of an Electric Vehicle

As a part of the Bristol Racing Society, I helped design and manufacture composite components for the single-seat car. These included the monocoque, lid and steering wheel. The car is set to be raced in the Greenpower 24+ Championship when complete.

We started by iterating the car design in Autodesk Fusion360 to try and make the monocoque as easy to manufacture as possible, whilst retaining the design features that the other teams requested.

We 3D printed sections of the vehicle and fused them together to form components. Using fibreglass, we created moulds around these plastic parts. Once complete, we inspected the moulds and used XCR coating resin to fill in any uneven sections where the fibreglass was too thin.

Above is a photo of what the fibreglass mould, with gel release coat looks like. On the right is the moulds for the monocoque and lid of the car.

Once complete, we inspected the moulds and used XCR coating resin to fill in any uneven sections where the fibreglass was too thin.

The large moulds, such as the monocoque and lid, we transported to the National Composites Centre (NCC) and the carbon fibre and jute layups were performed there.

The monocoque and lid were vacuum bagged and a drop test was performed before performing the infusion.

The process went very smoothly, as we planned all required steps meticulously and performed a risk assessment before attending.

Smaller components, such as the steering wheel (above), were resin infused locally.

Once infused, parts were post-processed. This included cutting off the outer flanges and smoothing the surface finish by removing remaining fibreglass and sanding. The mould was cut to shape (shown with the black taped lines on the car lid above) to allow for the driver to enter and for the wheel housings.

The next step combine the different chassis elements and integrate the steering and powertrain. When this is complete, the car can finally be raced!
